Frequently Asked Questions

  • If you're such a hot shot front-end developer, why is this site so crappy?

    I'm so glad you asked!

    Truthfully I long for the internet of my youth, the yet-to-be-monetized wild west of the 90s. Websites were often deliberately garish and it's an aesthetic I miss in these times of retina fonts and hero images. Every website back then was a labor of love.

    My goal for schwartz.world is to use it as a playground for my own experiments as a programmer. At work they expect pixel perfect, but that's not what you're going to find here. Instead my primary goals are as follows:

    • Accessibility - primarily accomplished through the use of semantic server-generated HTML
    • Speed - The vast majority of the content will be server-generated static assets. JS and CSS will be minimal and hand-coded.
    • Fun - For me to build and for you to consume

    If you feel like I've failed to meet any of those goals, I absolutely encourage you to drop in on The Repo and throw an issue or PR my way.

  • What is your background in development/programming?

    My first computer was an Apple IIe when I was 7. I learned a little BASIC, but never made anything of value. I didn't have learning materials beyond simple intro stuff for kids, and I was a little on the young side for self-learning something as dense as programming. Having a computer at such a young age did give me a leg up in computer literacy. I typed all my reports long before anyone I knew.

    My father bought an IBM clone in the 90s and got a second phone line so we could use AOL. They used to charge by the minute then. I racked up some minutes, let me tell you. I learned a little HTML, but I couldn't figure out how to make my sites go live. During this time I also took a course in Visual Basic and another in C++ but I didn't stick with either.

    In 2014, I taught myself Ruby using Michael Hartl's Rails Tutorial. Rails is an amazing framework, but you still have to understand front-end to make a good app, and this became the bottleneck for me pretty quickly. In 2015, I switched my focus to JavaScript and front-end development and the rest is history!
